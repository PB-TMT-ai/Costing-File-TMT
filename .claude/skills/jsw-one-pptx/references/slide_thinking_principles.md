## 6A. Slide Thinking — Consulting-Grade Construction Principles

**This section governs HOW slides are constructed, not how they look. Formatting (Sections 2-5) makes a slide brand-compliant. Slide thinking makes it land. The standard is: every slide should read like it was built by a principal at Bain, BCG, or McKinsey working with JSW One data.**

---

### Principle 1: One slide, one message

Every slide answers exactly one question. If a slide is trying to say two things, split it into two slides.

**Test:** Can you state the slide's message in one sentence? If not, the slide is doing too much.

| BAD | GOOD |
|-----|------|
| One slide showing volume targets, EBITDA bridge, AND risk flags together | Slide 1: Volume targets by BU. Slide 2: EBITDA bridge FY26 to FY27. Slide 3: Key risk flags on AOP delivery. |
| "Platform overview" slide with 8 metrics, 3 bullet sections, and a chart crammed together | Exec summary slide with 4 KPIs + 3 bullets. Separate detailed P&L slide. |

---

### Principle 2: Hybrid title approach — insight titles for key slides, topic titles for data slides

**Key slides (exec summary, section openers, recommendation slides, closing):** Use insight titles that state the conclusion. The reader should know the message from the title alone.

**Data slides (tables, detailed BU breakdowns, financial statements):** Use descriptive topic titles. The data speaks for itself.

| Slide type | Title approach | BAD title | GOOD title |
|-----------|---------------|-----------|------------|
| Exec summary | **Insight** | "Executive summary" | "FY27 marks the inflection — commerce turns EBITDA-positive" |
| Section takeaway | **Insight** | "Key takeaways from manufacturing" | "Manufacturing needs 950 new customer acquisitions to hit AOP" |
| Recommendation | **Insight** | "Recommendations for Private Brands" | "Dedicated service centers are the unlock for 1 Mn ton PB throughput" |
| Closing / ask | **Insight** | "Next steps" | "Three approvals needed from the Board to execute FY27 AOP" |
| Volume targets table | **Topic** | — | "FY27 AOP — volume targets by business unit" |
| P&L statement | **Topic** | — | "Consolidated P&L — Q3 FY26 actual vs AOP" |
| BU detail | **Topic** | — | "Private Brands — state-wise volume and dealer metrics" |
| Financial comparison | **Topic** | — | "Working capital metrics — FY24 to FY26 trend" |

**Rules:**
- Insight titles follow sentence case (same as all headings). They are concise, specific, and contain a number or fact where possible.
- An insight title should be 8-15 words. If it's longer, it's a paragraph, not a title.
- Never write an insight title that is vague: "Good progress this quarter" is not an insight. "Volume grew 35% but margin missed by 200 bps" is.
- When unsure, default to a topic title. A clear topic title is better than a forced insight title.

---

### Principle 3: Answer first, then support

The reader should get the conclusion from the top 20% of the slide (title + lead element). Supporting evidence fills the rest.

**Structure every slide top-down:**
```
[TITLE — the message or topic]
[LEAD — KPI cards, summary stat, or 1-line callout that delivers the punchline]
[SUPPORT — table, chart, bullets, or detail that backs up the lead]
[SOURCE — footer]
```

| BAD (bottom-up) | GOOD (top-down) |
|-----------------|-----------------|
| Long table of BU volumes at the top, then a small takeaway line at the bottom that says "PB missed by 30%" | Title: "Private Brands missed volume target by 30%". KPI card at top showing 525 KMT actual vs 751 KMT target. Supporting table below with state-wise breakdown. |
| 6 bullets of equal weight covering different topics | Title with the key point. First bullet is the critical one (bold). Remaining bullets are supporting detail in descending priority. |

---

### Principle 4: Every number earns its place

If a number on the slide doesn't support the slide's message, cut it. A table with 15 columns where only 4 matter is noise, not thoroughness. The rest belongs in an annexure.

| BAD | GOOD |
|-----|------|
| Full P&L with 20 line items on a board slide | 6-row summary P&L (GMV, Revenue, Gross Margin, CM1, Op EBITDA, PAT) on the board slide. Full P&L in annexure. |
| Competitor table showing 15 metrics including ones where data is "N/A" for 4 of 6 peers | 8 metrics where data exists for most peers. Clearly show "Data not available" only for genuine gaps, not empty columns. |
| BU slide showing all 12 months when the story is about Q3 | Q3 column, QoQ change, YoY change. Monthly detail in annexure. |

**Rule of thumb:** Main body slides should have 6-10 data points that all point to one conclusion. Annexure slides can have 20+.

---

### Principle 5: Visual hierarchy guides the eye

The reader's eye follows: title (largest text) then largest visual element (chart/KPI/image) then bold text then body text then footnotes. Design each slide so this scan path matches the logic path.

| Element | Role in scan path |
|---------|------------------|
| Slide title (20pt bold blue) | Reader sees this first. It frames everything below. |
| KPI cards / hero stat (28-36pt) | Eye jumps here second. This is the "headline number." |
| Chart / primary visual | Eye scans this next. This is the evidence. |
| Table / detail | Reader digs into this if interested. |
| Takeaway text / callout (12pt) | Confirms the message after seeing the data. |
| Footnotes (10pt grey) | Only read if reader questions the source. |

**Common hierarchy mistakes:**

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Takeaway text is larger or bolder than the chart | Takeaway supports the chart, not the other way around. Keep takeaway at 12pt. |
| Table dominates the slide but the actual message is in a small text box at the bottom | If the message is in the text, make it bigger (cards or callout). Shrink or simplify the table. |
| All elements are the same size | Create contrast. One element should dominate (60%+ of content area). |

---

### Principle 6: White space is not wasted space

An MBB slide with 3 sharp bullets and a clean chart beats a slide packed with 12 bullets and 3 tables. Resist the instinct to fill every pixel.

| BAD | GOOD |
|-----|------|
| 5 content blocks crammed into one slide with 0.05" gaps between them | 2-3 content blocks with 0.15-0.20" breathing room. Remaining content on the next slide. |
| Recommendation slide with 8 action items, 4 KPIs, and a table all on one slide | 3-4 action items as cards with clear owners. KPIs on a separate exec summary slide. |

**But:** White space at the bottom of a slide because the content is sparse is also a problem (per Section 6.4). The fix is NOT to add filler. It's to reconsider the layout (use cards instead of bullets, centre content vertically, expand the chart, or merge with another slide).

---

### Principle 7: Annotations beat separate takeaway boxes

Instead of a separate "Key takeaway" box below a chart, annotate directly on the visual. Circle a data point on the chart. Add a callout arrow to the critical bar. Put the insight inline.

| Approach | When to use |
|----------|------------|
| **Inline annotation** (callout on chart, bold cell in table, ▲▼ with color) | When the data tells a clear story and one data point is the highlight. The annotation draws the eye to it. |
| **Descriptive title** (insight title that states the conclusion) | When the title alone delivers the message and the visual is supporting evidence. |
| **Separate takeaway box** | Only when the insight is complex (2-3 sentences) and cannot be captured in a title or inline annotation. This is the last resort, not the default. |

| BAD | GOOD |
|-----|------|
| A chart showing volume growth with a separate box below saying "Volume grew 35%" | Chart with a callout arrow on the FY27 bar: "35% growth to 4,848 KMT". No separate takeaway box needed. |
| Table with all cells same formatting, plus a box below saying "PB missed target" | Table where the PB row's variance cell is in bold with ▼ -30% in grey. No separate box needed. |

---

### Principle 8: Slide sequencing tells a story

Slides aren't independent. The order creates a narrative arc. Every section should follow one of these patterns:

**Pattern A — Situation, Complication, Resolution (SCR):**
- Slide 1: "Here's where we are" (current state data)
- Slide 2: "Here's the problem" (gaps, misses, risks)
- Slide 3: "Here's what we're doing about it" (actions, priorities)

**Pattern B — Context, Performance, Outlook:**
- Slide 1: Market context or AOP targets
- Slide 2: Actual performance vs targets
- Slide 3: Forward outlook and adjustments

**Pattern C — Summary then Detail:**
- Slide 1: Executive summary with headline KPIs
- Slides 2-N: Detailed breakdowns by BU/topic
- Last slide: Synthesis and asks

**Each section divider should signal which pattern follows.** The subtitle on the section divider tells the reader what to expect: "Volume, revenue, and EBITDA performance across business units" sets up a Performance then Detail arc.

---

### Principle 9: Comparison is more powerful than absolute numbers

A number in isolation means nothing. "GMV was INR 18,590 Cr" is a fact. "GMV grew 48% to INR 18,590 Cr, 2.4x the growth rate of the nearest competitor" is insight.

**Every important metric should have at least one comparator:**

| Comparator | When to use | Example |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| vs AOP / target | Performance tracking | "Volume: 3,046 KMT (97% of AOP)" |
| vs previous period (QoQ/MoM/YoY) | Trend direction | "+35% YoY, +8% QoQ" |
| vs peer / benchmark | Competitive positioning | "NWC 12 days vs peer avg 54 days" |
| vs prior year same period | Seasonality-adjusted | "Q3 FY26 vs Q3 FY25: +41%" |

**In tables:** Always include at least one comparison column (growth %, variance %, vs target %). A table showing only absolute values for one period is incomplete.

**In KPI cards:** Always include a sublabel comparator: "4,848 KMT" alone is incomplete. "4,848 KMT | +59% vs FY26" is complete.

---

### Principle 10: Simplify for the audience, not for yourself

Board members have 3-5 minutes per slide. They need the signal, not the noise. Internal reviews for the CEO can be denser. Weekly reviews for the ops team can be data-heavy.

| Audience | Slide density | Detail level | Title style |
|---------|--------------|-------------|-------------|
| Board of Directors | 4-6 data points per slide | Summary metrics only. Detail in annexure. | Insight titles on key slides |
| CEO / leadership | 6-10 data points per slide | Intermediate. Show the "why" behind the numbers. | Mix of insight and topic |
| BU team / ops | 10-15 data points per slide | Dense. They know the context, show them the data. | Topic titles (they know the story) |

**Never build a board slide with the density of an ops review.** If the user says "this is for the Board", halve the content density compared to what feels complete. If they say "this is for my weekly with the CEO", keep it moderate. If they say "this is for the BU team S&OP", go data-heavy.
